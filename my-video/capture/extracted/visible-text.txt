Inherited IRAs Under the New Tax Rules (SECURE Act 2.0 / 10-Year Rule)

Topic: What happens when you inherit an IRA today, and how the rules changed.

Background:
- The SECURE Act (2019) eliminated the "stretch IRA" for most non-spouse beneficiaries. Before, a beneficiary could stretch withdrawals — and taxes — over their own life expectancy, sometimes decades.
- Now, most non-spouse beneficiaries fall into the "10-Year Rule": the entire inherited IRA must be emptied by December 31 of the 10th year after the original owner's death. There's no fixed annual withdrawal amount required — beneficiaries have flexibility in timing — but the account must hit zero by year 10.

Who is exempt from the 10-year rule ("Eligible Designated Beneficiaries"):
- Surviving spouses (can also roll the IRA into their own).
- Minor children of the original owner (10-year clock starts once they reach majority).
- Disabled or chronically ill individuals.
- Beneficiaries not more than 10 years younger than the original owner.
These groups can still stretch distributions over their own life expectancy.

The catch most people miss (2024/2025 IRS final regulations):
- If the original account owner died ON OR AFTER their Required Beginning Date (i.e., they had already started Required Minimum Distributions), non-eligible beneficiaries subject to the 10-year rule must ALSO take annual RMDs during years 1-9, not just empty the account by year 10.
- If the owner died BEFORE their Required Beginning Date, no annual RMDs are required during the 10 years — just empty the account by the end of year 10.
- Missing a required annual distribution used to trigger a 50% excise tax penalty on the shortfall. That penalty was reduced (SECURE 2.0) to 25%, and further to 10% if corrected within two years.

Roth vs. Traditional inherited IRAs:
- Traditional inherited IRA: every withdrawal is taxable income.
- Roth inherited IRA: withdrawals are generally tax-free, but the 10-year emptying rule still applies. No annual RMDs are required within the 10 years for Roth accounts regardless of the original owner's age, since Roth owners have no RBD.

Why this matters / planning angle:
- Waiting until year 10 to withdraw everything can push a beneficiary into a much higher tax bracket in one year.
- Smart planning spreads withdrawals across the 10-year window — especially in lower-income years — to minimize the tax hit.
- Beneficiaries should check whether they owe annual RMDs (based on the original owner's RMD status) to avoid the penalty entirely.

Call to action: Talk to a tax or financial advisor before deciding when to withdraw from an inherited IRA — timing changes how much of it you keep.
